Lampsilis bracteata (Gould, 1855)

Texas Fatmucket

 

 

Lampsilis bracteata – top image, female, Llano River [Colorado River drainage], length 41.0 mm; bottom image, male, Llano River [Colorado River drainage], length 42.0 mm.

 

Identification

Shell structure: thin to moderately thick, somewhat inflated.

 

Shell outline: elliptical to ovate.

 

Posterior ridge: low and broadly rounded to barely perceptible.

 

Shell color: yellowish-green, brownish-green, or brown; surface subglossy to glossy.

 

Rays: prominent, wavy, green or black broken rays that widen towards the margin.

 

Shell texture: smooth.

 

Umbo: low, broad and elevated above the hinge line; umbo cavity shallow to moderately deep.

 

Umbo sculpture: absent or consisting of fine double-looped or v-shaped ridges.

 

Pseudocardinal teeth: thin, triangular, 2 teeth in left valve, nearly parallel to the hinge line, may be slightly divergent or appear as a single tooth, anterior tooth larger than posterior, and 1 tooth in right valve.

Lateral teeth: relatively long, thin, nearly straight to slightly curved, 2 in left valve, 1 in right valve.

 

Interdentum: moderately long, narrow to nearly absent.

 

Nacre: white or bluish-white, may have a salmon or orange tint; iridescent posteriorly.

 

Other: sexually dimorphic, posterior margin broadly rounded to truncate in females, narrowly rounded to pointed in males; females tend to be more inflated posteriorly than males.

 

General Range

Endemic to tributaries of the Colorado River drainage of central Texas.

 

Habitat

Reported in small streams to medium-sized rivers, may penetrate into headwaters. Found in slow to moderate current in sand, mud, and gravel substrates among large cobble, boulders, bedrock ledges, horizontal cracks in bedrock slabs, and macrophyte beds. Has also been observed inhabiting the roots of cypress trees and vegetation along steep banks.

 

Legal listing status

USFWS: Candidate

Texas: State Threatened
