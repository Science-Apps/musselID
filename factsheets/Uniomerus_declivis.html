Uniomerus declivis (Say, 1831)

Tapered Pondhorn

 

Uniomerus declivis – Pecan Bayou [Red River drainage], length 70.7 mm.

 

Identification

Shell structure: thin to somewhat thick, moderately inflated.

 

Shell outline: subrhomboidal or trapezoidal and sharply pointed posteriorly.

 

Posterior ridge: high, rounded, often distinct, may show second or third ridge.

 

Shell color: tan, brown, or black; surface subglossy to dull, roughened or flaky.

 

Rays: none.

 

Shell texture: without sculpture.

 

Umbo: broad, low, elevated slightly above the hinge line; umbo cavity wide, shallow.

 

Umbo sculpture: single-looped bars, may look comma-shaped.

 

Pseudocardinal teeth: thin to moderately thick, 2 in the left valve, with anterior tooth angling downward and away from the anterior-dorsal margin, posterior tooth may be less well defined, 1 tooth in the right valve.

 

Lateral teeth: somewhat short, moderately thin, straight to slightly curved, 2 in left valve, 1 in right valve. 

 

Interdentum: long, narrow to absent.

 

Nacre: white, sometimes with purple tint; iridescent posteriorly

 

Other: not sexually dimorphic.

 

 

General range

Rio Grande and potentially northern Mexico to Mississippi, and potentially Alabama, and north to at least extreme southern Illinois.

 

Habitat

Streams, rivers, oxbows, marshes, swamps, lakes, canals, ponds, and reservoirs in still to moderate currents in mud, sand, or gravel substrates. Also found in woody debris such as logjams and exposed roots of riparian trees.

 

Legal listing status

USFWS: None
